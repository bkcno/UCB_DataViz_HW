The Numbers Behind Healthcare Coverage

Does Education Promote Health Awareness?

It is almost self explanatory that employement status impacts healthcare coverage. However, after analyzing various datasets from both American FactFinder and Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System comparing healthcare coverage, or lack thereof, against multiple variables, the results are not as simpl as it seems. When examing the year 2014 data across all 50 states, we have found that being employed full-time has negative 40% correlation with not having health insurance coverage, that is, being a full-time employee predicts having healthcare coverage with 40% accuracy. Poverty, on the other hand, has 55.66% positive correlation with the absence of healthcare coverage. Interesting enough, the correlations between education levels and healthcare coverage are more significant than these two factors. For the group with education of college or higher, the correlation with lack of healthcare is negative 56.92%, whereas, for group with less than high school graduate education, the correlation with lack of healthcare is at 68.95%! Hence, the finding here could enforce the argument that educational programs and policies are important public health instruments.

The Road Ahead:

Not surprisingl, there is a positive 68.26% correlation between lower education and poverty. Understanding the socialeconomical dynamic in between helps the general public to be better informed. The failure to provide social safety net to the struggling or vulnerable population will be in the long run costly to the society as whole.
